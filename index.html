Background

The Sand Creek Massacre (1864) and the Great Sioux War (1865/1867) exposed the growing conflict between U.S. westward expansion and Native American sovereignty. The Sand Creek Massacre, in which U.S. troops killed hundreds of Cheyenne and Arapaho people, shocked the nation and led to a congressional investigation. While few officials were held accountable, the event revealed serious failures in federal Indian policy and briefly raised calls for reform.

Instead of lasting change, the years that followed saw increased reliance on military force. During the Great Sioux War, the U.S. government repeatedly violated treaties and used armed conflict to secure land for settlers and railroads.
